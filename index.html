I am an Associate Professor with tenure in the Department of Political Science and Legal Studies at Suffolk University, Boston. I hold a Ph.D. in Government from Harvard University and a B.S. in Economic and Business Sciences from the University of Barcelona. My detailed curriculum vitae is available here.

My research focuses on political methodology, elections, and education, with a particular interest in estimating causal effects. Examples include: What are the effects of political TV ads on voter turnout? Do negative political TV ads influence voters differently than positive ones? What mechanisms underpin incumbents’ advantages in elections? How do specific forms of student collaboration impact academic performance? What are the outcomes of attending private versus public schools?

My latest publication, co-authored with Kosuke Imai, is Data Analysis for Social Science: A Friendly and Practical Introduction (Princeton University Press, 2022). This textbook provides an accessible introduction to survey research, predictive modeling, and causal inference, using the free and widely-used statistical software R.

Since 2016, I have taught Data Analysis and Politics (POLS 201), an undergraduate quantitative-methods course at Suffolk University. In this hands-on course, students learn R programming and statistical techniques to analyze real-world data and answer substantive political questions, such as the effect of Pfizer's vaccine on COVID-19 infection rates. The course emphasizes critical evaluation of social science studies.

In 2022–23, I had the honor of delivering the Convocation Speech at Suffolk University. In 2021–22, I received the Innovative Teaching Award for implementing creative teaching strategies in my quantitative-methods course. In 2020–21, I was nominated by Suffolk's Student Government Association for the Outstanding College of Arts and Sciences Faculty Member of the Year Award.

I am affiliated with Harvard University's Institute for Quantitative Social Science and the Center for American Political Studies. Previously, I served as a postdoctoral fellow in the Democracy Program at NYU School of Law's Brennan Center for Justice and as a visiting scholar at the Juan March Institute.
